The Magnificent 7 will spend approximately $750 billion in capital expenditure in 2026 — nearly double their 2025 combined spend of ~$440 billion. Four companies (Amazon, Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta) account for 93% of the total. Apple and Nvidia remain outliers: Apple's $13B commitment is smaller than Meta's single quarterly spend, while Nvidia — whose products absorb most of this capital — invests modestly in its own infrastructure.[1][2]

This is not speculative. Every figure below traces to an SEC filing, earnings call transcript, or official management guidance. Where estimates are required, methodology is explicit.

03

The Aggregate Picture

Mag7 Combined Capex ($B) — The Hockey Stick

The combined figure approached $1 trillion when including finance leases and infrastructure commitments beyond pure capex. Goldman Sachs projects $7.6 trillion cumulative AI CapEx from 2026-2031, starting at ~$765B/year and growing to $1.6T/year by 2031.[1]

Every major hyperscaler reported the same constraint on their Q1 2026 earnings calls: demand exceeds supply. Microsoft disclosed an $80B Azure backlog unfulfillable due to power constraints. Alphabet's cloud backlog nearly doubled QoQ to $460B. Amazon said AI capacity is "monetized as quickly as it is installed."[7][5][3]

Build vs. Buy: The Custom Silicon War

AI Accelerator Market: Custom ASICs vs GPUs — CAGR Through 2033

2026 marks the custom silicon inflection point. Every hyperscaler now has production-scale alternatives to Nvidia GPUs, primarily targeting inference workloads where cost-per-token economics dominate. Bloomberg Intelligence projects custom ASICs growing at 44.6% CAGR vs. 16.1% for GPUs through 2033.[15]

The Binding Constraint: Energy & Power

Power is now the primary bottleneck for AI infrastructure, not capital or chip supply. Microsoft disclosed an $80 billion Azure backlog that cannot be fulfilled due to power constraints. This is driving an unprecedented corporate rush into energy procurement — particularly nuclear.[7]

Combined: 10GW+ of new nuclear capacity contracted by Big Tech. For context, 10GW equals roughly 10 conventional nuclear reactors. This is the corporate world's largest-ever bet on nuclear energy — driven entirely by AI demand.[14]

Investment implication: The energy constraint creates a structural advantage for companies with secured power. It also creates massive tailwinds for: nuclear services companies, grid infrastructure, cooling technology, and energy storage. The ~$35-56B/year flowing into energy procurement will grow as AI inference demand scales.

Implications: What This Tells Us

Where They Converge

All four hyperscalers are doing the same thing: building massive GPU clusters (primarily Nvidia Blackwell), constructing data centers at unprecedented scale, securing nuclear/renewable power, and developing custom inference chips. The playbook is identical. The differentiator is execution speed and capital access.[12]

What This Means for Startups

The $750B creates opportunity in three layers:

  • Picks & shovels beneficiaries: Cooling technology, power infrastructure, fiber/interconnect, DC construction, liquid cooling (Vertiv, Schneider, Eaton)
  • Software for the infra layer: DC management, AI workload orchestration, energy optimization, supply chain visibility for chips
  • Application layer arbitrage: As compute becomes commodity via hyperscaler competition, application-layer AI companies benefit from falling inference costs without bearing the capex burden

09

The Meta Paradox

Meta is the most interesting case study in this analysis. It's spending $125-145B in 2026 — on par with Microsoft and Alphabet — but without a cloud business to directly monetize the infrastructure. Every other hyperscaler rents compute to external customers. Meta consumes it all internally.[6]

The bull case: Meta is building the infrastructure for "Meta Superintelligence Labs" and next-generation AI that will transform its $160B advertising business. The bear case: this is the Metaverse 2.0 — massive investment without clear revenue payback.

Critically, Meta's Q1 2026 capex came in at $19.84B — well below the $27.57B Street estimate. This suggests spending is heavily back-loaded into Q2-Q4, or the raised guidance ($125-145B) includes significant finance leases and infrastructure commitments beyond traditional capex.[13]

10

The Memory Crisis

Microsoft CFO Amy Hood explicitly stated that $25 billion of the $190B forecast comes from higher component prices — primarily HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) costs. This is not a Microsoft-specific problem. A global memory crunch driven by AI demand is inflating capex across all players.[7]

HBM demand for AI accelerators (used in both Nvidia GPUs and custom ASICs) has outpaced supply expansion from SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron. The result: 30-50% price increases in 2025-2026 that cascade through every data center buildout. Goldman Sachs identifies memory as one of the key assumptions that could push total AI capex above or below $7.6T.[1]
