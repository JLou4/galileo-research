Jevons' Paradox Applied to Intelligence

What happens when the cost of thinking drops 280x in two years? The same thing that happened when coal got cheap: everything changes.

📅 May 11, 2026 🔭 Galileo Research 🎯 Investment Thesis

Executive Summary

In 1865, William Stanley Jevons observed that James Watt's more efficient steam engine didn't reduce coal consumption — it increased it massively, because cheap energy made entirely new applications economical. One hundred sixty-one years later, the same paradox is playing out in artificial intelligence. The cost of GPT-3.5-level inference dropped from $20 to $0.07 per million tokens between November 2022 and October 2024 — a 280x collapse. [1] GPT-4-equivalent performance now costs $0.40/million tokens versus $20 in late 2022, a 50x decline. [2] Rather than shrinking the market, this collapse has detonated an explosion of demand.

  • The core mechanism: Efficiency doesn't just make existing uses cheaper — it makes new uses economically viable for the first time. Coal didn't just heat homes more cheaply; it created railroads, factories, and mass production. AI won't just automate existing workflows more cheaply; it will create categories of economic activity that couldn't exist when intelligence was scarce.
  • Demand is super-elastic: Zhang & Zhang (2026) formalize this as a "Structural Jevons Paradox" — as inference costs fall, developers don't just run the same workloads more cheaply; they redesign agent architectures to consume dramatically more compute (deeper reasoning, larger contexts, multi-agent workflows). [3]
  • We're already seeing it: AI capex hit $650B (~2% of GDP) in 2026. Software engineer job postings are up 11% YoY despite AI automation concerns. Total token consumption is growing faster than per-token costs are falling. [4]
  • The historical pattern is consistent: Coal → steam → railroads → suburbs → mass consumption. Electricity → appliances → suburbs → two-income households. Internet → e-commerce → gig economy → creator economy. Every major cost collapse in a foundational input created categories that were inconceivable before the collapse.

Bottom line for investors: The biggest returns from AI will not come from making existing processes cheaper (substitution effects). They will come from entirely new markets that become viable only when intelligence is essentially free — markets that don't exist yet and that most investors aren't imagining. Jevons' Paradox is the framework for finding them.

1. The Mechanics: Coal to Intelligence

How Jevons Works

The paradox operates through a simple mechanism with non-obvious consequences:

  1. Efficiency improvement reduces the cost per unit of a resource
  2. Direct rebound: Existing users consume more (cheaper = use more of it)
  3. Indirect rebound: Savings are redirected to other activities that also consume the resource
  4. Structural expansion: Entirely new use cases become economically viable — uses that were inconceivable at the old price point
  5. When the combined demand increase exceeds the efficiency gain, total consumption rises

Jevons' original observation: Watt's steam engine reduced coal per unit of work by ~75%. But cheap mechanical energy made steam viable for textile mills, iron smelting, pumping, transportation, and hundreds of other applications. UK coal consumption rose from 16 million tons in 1829 to 72 million tons in 1860 — a 4.5x increase during a period when per-unit efficiency was improving dramatically. [5]

Why AI Demand Is Super-Elastic

Zhang & Zhang's January 2026 paper formalizes what practitioners already observe: falling inference costs don't just lower bills — they change architectures. [3] At $20/million tokens, you call the API once with a simple prompt. At $0.07/million tokens, you:

  • Run 10 parallel chains of reasoning and pick the best answer
  • Give the agent a 200K-token context window instead of 4K
  • Deploy multi-agent systems where agents debate, critique, and refine each other's work
  • Run continuous background agents that monitor, learn, and act 24/7
  • Process every customer interaction, every document, every data point — not just the important ones

This is the "Structural Jevons Paradox": the production function itself changes in response to lower prices. It's as if cheaper coal didn't just let factories run longer, but caused factories to spontaneously redesign themselves into entirely new configurations.

The key insight for investors: The substitution effect (AI replaces human task X) is well-understood and already priced into markets. The Jevons effect (AI enables entirely new category Y that was inconceivable before) is where the alpha lives. Every major technology transition has created more value from new categories than from substitution. AI will be no different.

3. Entirely New Categories

The Industrial Revolution didn't just make existing things cheaper — it created categories of economic activity that were literally inconceivable before cheap energy. Railroads weren't "faster horses." Department stores weren't "bigger markets." Mass media wasn't "louder town criers." Each was a genuinely new category that emerged from the structural reorganization of society around cheap energy.

What are the AI equivalents?

Categories That Don't Exist Yet

1. The Cognitive Utility. Today, intelligence is a product (you buy a tool, hire a person, or call an API). When intelligence costs approach zero, it becomes a utility — always on, always available, metered like electricity. You don't "use AI" any more than you "use electricity." It's simply the substrate on which everything runs. The business model shifts from selling intelligence to selling outcomes enabled by intelligence. Just as nobody buys kilowatt-hours for fun — they buy lighting, cooling, computation — nobody will buy tokens for fun. They'll buy "my taxes done correctly" or "my factory running optimally."

2. Synthetic Relationships at Scale. Not chatbots. Not virtual assistants. Persistent, evolving, context-rich relationships between humans and AI entities that accumulate shared history over years. An AI financial advisor that has watched your spending patterns for a decade. An AI mentor that has tracked your career for 20 years. An AI health companion that knows your complete medical history, genetics, and daily biometrics. These relationships create value through accumulated context that is expensive to replicate — creating switching costs and moats that look more like human relationships than software subscriptions.

3. The Micro-Enterprise Economy. Today, starting a business requires a minimum team: at least a builder, a seller, a and an operator. When AI can fill all three roles, the minimum viable team drops to one human with a vision. We're not talking about solopreneurs with AI tools — we're talking about one person running a 100-"employee" operation where all employees are AI agents. The fixed costs of business drop by 10-100x, which means businesses become viable at revenue levels ($10K-$100K/year) that couldn't support a traditional company. The economy fragments into millions of micro-enterprises, each serving a niche too small for traditional businesses to notice.

4. Ambient Intelligence Environments. Physical spaces that think. Not smart homes with voice assistants — environments that continuously model their occupants, predict needs, and adapt without being asked. A hospital room that adjusts lighting, medication schedules, and nurse call priority based on real-time patient monitoring. A factory floor that reconfigures workflows minute-by-minute based on demand signals, equipment health, and worker fatigue patterns. A city block that optimizes traffic flow, energy use, and emergency response simultaneously. The "smart city" concept, currently stalled in pilot projects, becomes economically viable when the intelligence layer costs essentially nothing to run.

5. Automated Discovery Engines. Scientific research today is bottlenecked by human attention: researchers can only read so many papers, run so many experiments, and test so many hypotheses. When AI can read every paper ever published, generate novel hypotheses, design experiments, and interpret results — the rate of scientific discovery accelerates by orders of magnitude. This isn't AI "replacing" scientists; it's AI expanding the frontier of what can be investigated. The number of scientifically interesting questions has always been infinite; human attention was the binding constraint. Remove the constraint, and discovery becomes a continuous, automated process rather than an episodic, human-paced one.

6. Real-Time Regulatory Compliance. Today, compliance is retrospective: you hire lawyers and auditors to check if you followed the rules after the fact. When intelligence is cheap, compliance becomes prospective and continuous. Every business transaction, every employee action, every product shipment is evaluated against every applicable regulation in real-time — across every jurisdiction the company operates in. The compliance industry ($55B globally) doesn't shrink; it transforms from periodic auditing to continuous assurance. The new category isn't "cheaper compliance" — it's "impossible to be non-compliant."

7. Negotiation as a Service. Every economic transaction involves negotiation — price, terms, timing, quality. Humans negotiate a tiny fraction of their transactions (car purchases, salary, home buying) because negotiation is cognitively expensive and socially awkward. When AI can negotiate every purchase, every contract, every subscription renewal, every insurance claim — consumers capture billions in value they currently leave on the table. A market emerges for AI agents that negotiate on your behalf, taking a percentage of savings. This doesn't exist today because the "thinking cost" of negotiation exceeded the value for small transactions. At $0.01/negotiation, it's worth negotiating everything.

The test for "genuinely new category" vs. "cheaper existing thing": Can you explain it to someone in 2020 using only concepts they already know? If yes, it's substitution. If you need new language, new mental models, new regulatory frameworks — it's a new category. Department stores required the concept of "browsing" (shopping without a specific purchase intent). Railroads required the concept of "timetables" (coordinating activity across distances by synchronized time). The AI equivalents will require concepts we haven't named yet.

4. Historical Parallel Chains

Chain 1: Coal (1765-1900)

Watt's efficient steam engine (1765) → cheap mechanical energy → factories relocate from rivers to cities → urbanization → railroads (1825) → national markets → mass production → department stores (1858) → consumer culture → advertising industry → mass media

Key insight: 135 years from Watt's engine to mass media. Each link was non-obvious from the previous one. Nobody building steam engines predicted department stores. The causal chain was: cheap energy → urbanization → national logistics → mass production → consumer surplus → new retail formats → new media. [8] [9]

Chain 2: Electricity (1882-1960)

Edison's Pearl Street Station (1882) → electric lighting → extended working hours → electric motors in factories (1900s) → assembly line (1913) → consumer appliances (1920s-1950s) → household labor savings → women enter workforce → two-income households → suburban expansion → automobile dependence

Key insight: The washing machine's impact on the labor force was more consequential than the electric motor's impact on factories. The second and third-order effects (household time savings → women's workforce participation → suburban growth) were larger than the first-order effect (cheaper factory power). Nobody electrifying Pearl Street predicted the suburban revolution.

Chain 3: Internet (1995-2020)

Netscape browser (1995) → cheap information distribution → e-commerce (Amazon, 1995) → disintermediation of retail → smartphones (2007) → always-on connectivity → gig economy (Uber, 2009) → algorithmic labor markets → creator economy (YouTube monetization, Instagram, TikTok) → individual as media company

Key insight: The internet made information distribution cheap. The non-obvious consequence was that it made coordination cheap — which enabled gig work, creator economies, and eventually individual media empires. The "creator economy" ($250B+ by 2025) was inconceivable in 1995. The internet didn't make existing media companies cheaper; it made everyone a potential media company.

ChatGPT / GPT-4 (2022-2023) → cheap cognitive work → AI coding tools (2024) → 10-100x more software created → micro-enterprise explosion → AI agents as employees (2025-2026) → every person manages a "team" of AI agents → individual as enterprise → [?] → [?] → [?]

Projected next links:

  • 2026-2028: "One-person enterprise" becomes a recognized category. Individual operators running AI agent teams generate $1-10M revenue. The minimum viable company drops from 10 people to 1.
  • 2028-2030: Agent-to-agent commerce emerges. AI agents negotiate, transact, and fulfill on behalf of humans. The "economy" includes non-human participants with purchasing power, creating markets between agents. (This is where our earlier research on agent-to-agent commerce — Circuit & Chisel — connects.)
  • 2030-2035: Physical world integration. AI agents that can think cheaply + robotics that can act cheaply = intelligence embedded in the physical environment. Every object, surface, and space becomes intelligent. The built environment reorganizes around ambient intelligence, just as cities reorganized around electricity.
  • 2035+: The equivalent of "suburban expansion" — a structural reorganization of how humans live and work that is non-obvious from today's vantage point. If the pattern holds, the biggest consequence of cheap intelligence will be something we haven't named yet.
The humility check: If someone in 1882 had tried to predict the consequences of cheap electricity, they would have predicted "brighter streets" and "safer factories." They would not have predicted the washing machine, the two-income household, the suburb, or the automobile. Our predictions for AI are probably similarly limited. The most honest version of Chain 4 ends with "[categories we cannot currently name]."

5. Investable Implications

The Jevons Investment Framework

If Jevons' Paradox is the correct mental model, the investment implications are specific and contrarian:

1. Bet on demand expansion, not cost reduction. Most AI investment today is focused on making existing processes cheaper (substitution). The Jevons framework says the larger opportunity is in new demand creation. Companies building for markets that don't yet exist — but will at 100x cheaper intelligence — are the analogues of railroad companies in 1825.

2. The "intelligence infrastructure" layer is the highest-conviction bet. In every Jevons cycle, the picks-and-shovels companies win regardless of which specific applications emerge. Coal mines and railroad steel won whether the application was textile mills or department stores. For AI:

  • Compute infrastructure (NVIDIA, cloud providers) — already priced in
  • Inference optimization (cost reduction = demand expansion flywheel) — still venture-scale opportunities
  • Agent orchestration (managing thousands of AI agents per organization) — nascent category
  • Trust/verification infrastructure (when intelligence is cheap, trust becomes scarce) — early innings
  • Intelligence metering/billing (the "utility meter" for cognitive work) — barely exists

3. Look for the "below the threshold" markets. The most valuable Jevons investments are in activities that were just barely uneconomical before the cost drop. These are the first to become viable. Right now:

  • Legal services for disputes involving $500-$5,000 (currently below the cost floor for attorney involvement)
  • Personalized education for children in families earning $30-80K (can't afford tutors, can't afford to not have them)
  • Financial advisory for people with $10K-$100K in assets (below the minimums for human advisors)
  • R&D for markets of 10K-100K potential customers (below the threshold for traditional pharmaceutical/product development)
  • Professional services for businesses with $500K-$5M revenue (too small for McKinsey, too complex for DIY)

4. The "cognitive utility" company is the trillion-dollar opportunity. Just as Standard Oil (energy utility) and AT&T (communication utility) became the dominant companies of their eras by providing foundational inputs as metered services, the company that becomes the "cognitive utility" — always-on intelligence, metered and billed per unit of thinking delivered — will be one of the most valuable enterprises in history. Anthropic and OpenAI are racing toward this. But the utility model also creates opportunities for infrastructure plays (billing, governance, reliability) that every utility needs.

5. The scarcity inversion. In every Jevons cycle, when one resource becomes abundant, an adjacent resource becomes newly scarce. When energy became cheap, materials (steel, copper) became scarce. When information became cheap, attention became scarce. When intelligence becomes cheap, what becomes scarce?

  • Trust and verification — when anyone can generate convincing text, images, and code, proving authenticity becomes precious
  • Human judgment for novel situations — AI handles the 95% of cases that fit patterns; humans handle the 5% that don't. That 5% becomes more valuable per hour, not less.
  • Physical-world execution — thinking is cheap; doing is still expensive. Robotics, construction, and physical service delivery become the bottleneck.
  • Attention and curation — infinite content generation → human attention is the binding constraint → curation and recommendation become the value layer
  • Regulation and liability frameworks — new capabilities create new liability questions faster than legal systems can resolve them. Compliance, insurance, and governance become growth industries.

Back the companies building for markets that other investors think don't exist — because until intelligence was cheap, they didn't.
