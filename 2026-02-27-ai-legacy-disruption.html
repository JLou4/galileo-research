1. The Disruption Vulnerability Framework

Not all industries are equally vulnerable to AI. The factors that predict disruption potential are structural, not just technical:

  • Information intensity: How much of the work involves processing, analyzing, or generating information vs. physical manipulation?
  • Fragmentation: How fragmented is the industry? Fragmented industries (many small players, no dominant platform) are ripe for AI-powered consolidation.
  • Regulation density: Counter-intuitively, heavy regulation increases disruption potential ‚Äî compliance is repetitive, rule-based work that AI handles well, and the cost of compliance creates willingness to pay.
  • Labor intensity / labor scarcity: Industries facing structural labor shortages (construction, agriculture, government) have the strongest pull for automation.
  • Data availability: Industries that generate structured digital data are faster to disrupt; industries with messy, physical-world data take longer but face equally large eventual disruption.

A critical insight from Goldman Sachs: despite $258.7 billion in AI venture capital in 2025, AI's contribution to U.S. GDP growth was "basically zero."[3] Over 80% of companies report no productivity gains from AI so far. This isn't a failure of AI ‚Äî it's a failure of application. Capital has flowed to horizontal tools and foundation models while the vertical application layer ‚Äî where AI actually touches industry workflows ‚Äî remains underdeveloped.

TrueBridge Capital's analysis (Forbes, Feb 2026) confirms the shift: "If 2024 and 2025 were about foundation models, 2026 is increasingly about application-layer execution."[4] Vertical AI companies like Harvey (legal) and Abridge (healthcare) are demonstrating growth curves dramatically compressed compared to prior software generations ‚Äî reaching hundreds of millions in ARR within 2‚Äì3 years of launch.

3. The Gap Zones: Where Founders Aren't Building

üèõÔ∏è Government & Public Sector ‚Äî The Largest Gap

The U.S. government alone spends $8.9 trillion annually. State and local governments process millions of permits, licenses, benefits applications, and compliance reviews ‚Äî overwhelmingly through manual, paper-based workflows. AI adoption is in early pilot stages at best.[8]

Why it's underserved: Government procurement is slow, opaque, and relationship-driven. Sales cycles are 12‚Äì24 months. Founders prefer faster-moving enterprise markets. But the structural characteristics scream disruption: massive data volumes, repetitive rule-based decisions, chronic staffing shortages, and immense political pressure to modernize.

Who's building: Almost nobody at scale. Polimorphic ($18.6M Series A, 2025) sells AI chatbots and permitting tech to local governments.[8] EffiGov (YC) is building an "AI OS for local governments" starting with 311 call operators.[10] Deloitte published a major GovTech Trends 2026 report focused on "digital agents" for citizen services.[11] But compared to the ~$4.3B flowing into legaltech, government AI is essentially unfunded.

The opportunity:

  • 1st Order AI automates permit review, benefits adjudication, compliance checking ‚Äî reducing processing times from weeks to hours
  • 2nd Order Faster permitting unlocks construction, housing, and infrastructure projects currently bottlenecked by bureaucratic backlog ‚Äî multiplier effects across the economy
  • 3rd Order AI-enabled government services become the new standard of citizen expectation, making non-AI-enabled municipalities uncompetitive for talent and business attraction

üèóÔ∏è Construction ‚Äî The Physical-World Frontier

Construction is the world's largest industry at $13 trillion globally, and one of the least digitized. Meaningful AI adoption is below 10%.[7] AI in construction is expected to grow from $1.3 billion in 2022 to $13.5 billion by 2030 ‚Äî a 10x expansion that's still a fraction of the industry's size.[12]

Why it's underserved: Construction data is messy, fragmented, and physical-world. Unlike legal or finance, you can't just throw an LLM at a corpus of text. You need integration with sensors, drones, BIM models, project management tools, and safety systems. The technical bar is higher, and the customer base (general contractors, trade subcontractors) is notoriously resistant to new technology.

The opportunity:

  • Estimating & bidding: Construction bids are labor-intensive, error-prone, and based on experience rather than data. AI-powered estimating that learns from historical project data is a clear wedge.
  • Safety monitoring: Jobsite safety violations are a $15B+ annual cost. Computer vision + AI for real-time safety monitoring is technically ready but barely deployed.
  • Project scheduling: AI that can optimize complex project schedules with dynamic constraints, weather, supply chain delays ‚Äî replacing the current art of Gantt chart management.
  • Quality & compliance: AI-powered inspection of work quality against plans and code requirements, using drone and camera data.

The Contrarian Bet: Physical-World AI

The consistent pattern across the highest-gap industries (government, construction, agriculture, energy) is that they involve physical-world complexity. Most AI capital has flowed to information-first industries ‚Äî legal, finance, healthcare documentation ‚Äî where the data is clean and digital.

2nd Order Physical-world AI is harder to build, which means it's harder to compete against. A legal AI startup faces the risk that OpenAI or Google ships a competitive feature in a model update. A construction AI startup that integrates with drones, BIM systems, project management tools, and safety hardware has a defensibility moat that model improvements can't easily replicate.

3rd Order Physical-world AI unlocks GDP growth that information-only AI can't. Goldman Sachs says AI has added "basically zero" to GDP so far.[3] That's because information-worker productivity gains are real but small in economic terms. The big GDP unlock comes when AI makes physical processes more efficient ‚Äî construction, manufacturing, agriculture, energy. The $7 trillion GDP boost Goldman predicted requires AI to reach the physical economy. The companies that bridge that gap will capture enormous value.

The seed investor's edge: VCs with patience and domain networks can access these high-gap sectors before the generalist capital arrives. Construction, government, and energy have long sales cycles that deter impatient capital ‚Äî which is exactly what creates durable early-mover advantage. By the time the sector heats up, well-positioned seed investments will have 18‚Äì24 months of customer learnings, data, and workflow integration that later entrants can't replicate.

6. Key Risks & Open Questions

Why This Thesis Could Be Wrong

  • Adoption velocity may be overstated: MIT found that 95% of generative AI pilot projects at companies fail.[7] Physical-world industries may be even harder to crack than this analysis suggests. The gap might not be a market opportunity ‚Äî it might be evidence that these industries are structurally resistant to AI.
  • Platform bundling risk: Microsoft (via Copilot + government contracts), Salesforce (via Agentforce), and Autodesk (via construction tools) could bundle AI into existing platform relationships, squeezing vertical startups. Microsoft's free Copilot rollout to GSA covering millions of government employees is a direct threat.[18]
  • Regulatory backlash: High-profile AI failures in government (wrongful benefit denials, biased enforcement) could trigger restrictions that slow adoption rather than accelerate it.
  • The labor displacement backlash: TechCrunch's investor survey shows VCs expect AI to meaningfully impact the labor market in 2026.[19] If this manifests as visible job losses in legacy industries, political pushback could constrain adoption in precisely the sectors identified here.

Open Questions

  • Can vertical AI companies maintain pricing power as foundation models commoditize? Harvey's path to $8B suggests yes ‚Äî workflow integration creates switching costs ‚Äî but the jury is out for earlier-stage categories.
  • Will the "AI megacycle" concentration of capital at the top (a16z, Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins backing Harvey, etc.) crowd out seed investors, or create a vacuum at the earliest stages?
  • How do you measure AI ROI in industries that can't yet quantify their inefficiency? Construction companies don't know how much they lose to scheduling errors. Government agencies don't measure permit processing cost per unit. The startups that solve the measurement problem may be as valuable as the ones that solve the automation problem.
